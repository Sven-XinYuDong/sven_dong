Supervised Study on Heart Disease

2023
Xinyu Dong
Logistic Regression · Decision Trees
Introduction

Heart disease is a leading cause of death worldwide. Understanding risk factors may help prevent its development by enabling medical practitioners to identify susceptible individuals and apply early preventative interventions.

The current investigation examines three research questions:

  1. What lifestyle variables are related to the diagnosis of heart disease?
  2. What variables are most important to the diagnosis of heart disease?
  3. What groups of people are most likely to develop heart disease?

Questions 1 and 2 were investigated using logistic regression. Question 3 was investigated using decision trees.

Dataset

Data was obtained from Kaggle (Pytlak, 2022), a subset of CDC telephone interviews with 401,958 US residents across all 50 states. After filtering for heart disease-related variables, removing missing values, and eliminating 18,078 duplicates, the final dataset comprised 301,717 observations with 17 lifestyle predictor variables (4 numeric, 13 categorical).

Logistic Regression

Model Fitting

Interaction terms for AgeCategory × Sex and AgeCategory × Race were added based on EDA. The full model was then reduced using backward stepwise selection (AIC), removing PhysicalHealth, MentalHealth, and PhysicalActivity.

Model Diagnostics

Cook's distance identified a high number of influential points — generally observations with outlier values such as BMI of 40 (mean = 28, SD = 6). This violates the assumption of no influential points in logistic regression.

Fig 1. — Cook's distance plot showing influential observations during model fit.

Key Findings

Q1 — Variables related to heart disease: 15 of 17 variables were significant in the final model, including BMI, Smoking, Stroke, DiffWalking, Sex, AgeCategory, Race, Diabetic, GenHealth, SleepTime, Asthma, KidneyDisease, and SkinCancer.

Decision Trees

Model Fitting

A decision tree classifier was fitted using 10-fold cross validation to find the optimal node size. The process was repeated iteratively on high-risk subgroups until heart disease probability exceeded 50%.

Fig 2. — Optimal decision tree (5 nodes) for the full dataset.

The first tree (5 nodes) showed that age under 59 with good general health predicted absence of heart disease. Those over 59 with poor or fair health formed the high-risk group (31.10% probability vs 9.03% baseline).

Fig 3. — Second-stage decision tree fitted on the high-risk subgroup.

In the second iteration on the high-risk group, prior stroke history emerged as a key splitter. Those in the high-risk group with stroke history had a 50.67% probability of heart disease.

Conclusion

15 lifestyle variables were found to be associated with heart disease development. Age and general health were the strongest predictors. The decision tree identified people over 60, in poor or fair health, with a history of stroke as the highest-risk group (50.67% probability).

Limitations include a low adjusted R² (~0.2), a high number of influential points violating logistic regression assumptions, and imbalanced response variables. Future work should consider GAMs and oversampling techniques to address these issues.
