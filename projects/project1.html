Oreoscoptes Distribution in BC

2023
Xinyu Dong
Species Distribution Modelling
Introduction

Wildlife preservation in British Columbia (BC) is an important topic to address. Having diverse wildlife could lead to a number of benefits, such as thriving ecosystems (e.g. diverse species lead to more balanced and resilient ecosystems; Ferrero & Troìa, 2021), or economic benefits through nature tourism.

The Sage Thrasher (Oreoscoptes montanus) has experienced up to a 50% population decline over the past century in BC, and has been designated as endangered by BC Ministry of Environment. The current investigation aims to preserve sage thrasher conservation by conducting a spatial statistical analysis, focusing on three questions:

  • The spatial distribution of sage thrashers in BC.
  • The environmental covariates linked to sage thrasher population.
  • Regression models to predict geographical intensity and identify suitable new habitat areas.
Methods

Data

Occurrence data (850 BC sightings) was downloaded from GBIF (2023). Environmental covariates — elevation, distance to water, forest cover, and HFI — were obtained from BC-wide rasters provided by Michael Noonan (Noonan, 2022), ranging from 1M to 7M spatial measurements.

Analytical Workflow

1 — Data Preparation

Occurrence coordinates were cleaned for missing values and projected into a consistent CRS. A spatial point pattern object (ppp) was constructed within the BC observation window defined by as.owin(DATA$Window).

2 — Spatial Analysis

Homogeneity was assessed via quadrat test. Hot spot analysis identified areas of elevated intensity using kernel density estimation.

3 — Covariate Linkage

Relationships between each covariate and intensity were examined using rhohat kernel estimation. Forest and HFI were excluded due to wide bandwidths; Elevation and Distance to Water were selected as primary covariates.

4 — Regression Models

Both covariates were scaled by mean and variance. A Poisson point process model (PPM) was fit; due to convergence failure, the squared elevation term was dropped. An advanced GAM with 3 degrees of freedom per covariate was subsequently fitted.

Results

Spatial Distribution

The data is significantly inhomogeneous — sage thrashers cluster in southern BC, with the hotspot identified at Osoyoos. A quadrat test confirmed non-homogeneity (small p-value).

Environmental Covariates

Kernel estimation revealed two elevation clusters (0m and 500m) non-linearly related to bird intensity. Distance to water shows a preference band at 3–5 km from water sources.

Regression Models

The final PPM model retained scaled elevation and distance to water (with quadratic water term). All coefficients were statistically significant. The intensity function is:

λ(u) = exp(β₀ + β₁·Elevation_scaled + β₂·Dist_Water_scaled + β₃·Dist_Water_scaled²)

The model predicted Vancouver Island and Graham Island as potentially suitable new habitat areas, though no true observations fall within these zones.

GAM Extension

A GAM with 3 degrees of freedom per covariate was fitted, but spline terms still could not fully capture trends in the data.

Discussion

Sage thrashers are disproportionately concentrated in southern BC, with key habitat predictors being low elevation (0 and 500m) and distance to water (3–5 km). Models predict Vancouver Island and Graham Island as potentially suitable new habitat areas, though model diagnostics suggest these should be interpreted with caution.

Limitations include small sample size (~800 occurrences), a restricted covariate set (notably excluding weather), and model fit issues with both PPM and GAM approaches. Future work should incorporate larger samples and richer environmental predictors.
